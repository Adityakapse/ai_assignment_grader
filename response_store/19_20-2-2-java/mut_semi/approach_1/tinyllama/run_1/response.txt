Reversed Polish Notation (Postfix Notation) for Mathematical Expressions

A special notation is used for mathematical expressions, where the numbers are given first and then operators. This notation is called "Reversed Polish Notation" or "Postfix Notation". Here's how it works:
- The notation starts with a stack of numbers, represented by parentheses, with each number on its own line (left to right).
- The operator is first put on the stack, followed by two numbers separated by a space. In this notation, there are no left operands or right operands. This means that if an operator is encountered, it will be treated as a single value.
- If an operator is done, either (1) it will pop the operator from the stack and push the corresponding result on top of the stack (in reverse order), or (2) if the operator can't be executed without first being done, then it will do so by pushing the second number on to the stack and popping the left operand, followed by the right operand, and finally pushing the final result on the stack.
- The notation is processed from bottom up, with operators going in order of increasing precedence (highest to lowest). The last value on the stack represents the result of the operation.
- In this method, the stack is a simple list of numbers, and all values are converted to integers before being pushed onto the stack.
